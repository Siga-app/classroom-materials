Poetry Mentor Text

“First They Came”

By Martin Niemöller, 1946

Who was Martin Niemöller?

Martin Niemöller was a German pastor and Holocaust survivor. He wrote this after World War II as a reflection on silence, responsibility, and speaking out.

Read like writers

As we read, notice the speaker, repeated structure, anaphora, line breaks, and the way the final stanza changes the meaning of the whole poem.

We will read the poem across the next slides so we can preserve its structure and analyze it carefully.

Mentor Text · Part 1

“First They Came”

First they came for the Communists
And I did not speak out
Because I was not a Communist

Then they came for the Socialists
And I did not speak out
Because I was not a Socialist

What pattern is beginning to form?

Mentor Text · Part 2

“First They Came”

Then they came for the trade unionists
And I did not speak out
Because I was not a trade unionist

Then they came for the Jews
And I did not speak out
Because I was not a Jew

How does the repetition change as the poem continues?

Mentor Text · Final Turn

“First They Came”

Then they came for me
And there was no one left
To speak out for me.

Final turn: What changes when the speaker becomes the target?

Poetry Craft

Repetition, anaphora, and repeated image are related, but not identical.

Keep the poem slide open in your mind. We will use the same poem to name the craft moves before you draft on your Poetry Drafting Page.

Repetition

Any word, phrase, sound, or structure that appears more than once to create emphasis.

Anaphora

A specific kind of repetition where several lines begin the same way.

Repeated Image

An object or visual idea that returns, such as a photograph, a casket, the sea, or darkness.

As writers, we repeat on purpose. We repeat to make the reader feel the weight of an idea.

Poetry Task

Write from inside a moment.

Your poem must include:

  • A clear speaker
  • At least 8 lines
  • Repetition, anaphora, or a repeated image
  • At least 2 spelling words

Possible speakers:

  • Annemarie
  • Ellen
  • Mama or Papa
  • A person watching silently
  • A symbolic voice, like fear or courage
