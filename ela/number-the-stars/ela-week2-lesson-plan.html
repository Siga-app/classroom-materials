Spelling Words + Novel Study: Chapter 5 — "Who Is the Dark-Haired One?"

LP: Caring · Principled ATL: Thinking — Inference NtS Chapter 5 ELA.5.R.1.1 · ELA.5.V.1.1
Learning Intention

I will make inferences about characters' choices under pressure and connect spelling words from the novel to the story so that I understand them in context.

Warm-up — Digital Flashcards + Prediction Sort · All 10 Week 2 Words
Open the digital flashcards and preview all 10 spelling words. These words appear somewhere in Chapters 5–10. You'll meet each one in context as you read. After the flashcards: with a partner, predict which 3 are in Chapter 5. Write your predictions — and your reasoning — on your whiteboard.
imperiousinstitutionterrifiedphotographsuspiciousfishermanmeadowmourningcasketscattered
📌 Pattern to notice this week: imperious · suspicious both end in -ious. That suffix often signals an adjective describing a quality or attitude. Look for how each one is used.
What does Papa's lie to the Nazi soldiers tell us about who he is — and about what the occupation has made necessary?
Why does Annemarie reach for Ellen's necklace while Ellen is terrified? What is each girl afraid of?
What does it mean when a government closes a synagogue — what right is being taken away, and from whom?
Materials📖 Number the Stars — Chapter 5 · ELA notebooks🔤 Spelling Practice Page
Today we begin our Week 2 spelling work by previewing the words as readers, not just as spellers. These words come from Chapters 5 to 10, and each one will help us understand danger, identity, protection, setting, grief, and courage in the novel. As we read Chapter 5, we will think about what happens when a person’s identity becomes dangerous under an unjust system. We will pay close attention to how Annemarie’s family protects Ellen, how fear shows up in the scene, and how an object like a photograph can become evidence, protection, and risk all at once. By the end of class, you will use the chapter to explain how responsibility can look different when people are under pressure.
Learner ProfileCaring — Papa and Mama's choices in this chapter are acts of radical care, at great personal risk. The question "what does caring look like when it costs you something?" runs through the entire chapter.
Principled — Papa lies. It's worth sitting with that: this is a principled man doing something dishonest to protect something more important than honesty. That tension is the moral core of the chapter.
ATL Skill — Thinking: InferenceStudents practice reading between the lines: what does Annemarie's hesitation tell us about what she fears? What does Papa's steady voice tell us about his experience? Inference moves students from what happens to what it means.
UOI Connection — ResponsibilityThe closing of the synagogue is a concrete act of government taking away a community's right to gather. Connect explicitly: "We've been studying what governments do and what they owe their citizens. What LOI does this connect to?"
Lesson Flow

0:00 — Digital Flashcards + Prediction Sort

5 min
  • Open the digital flashcards and move quickly through all 10 spelling words. Students read each word aloud, notice any tricky spelling pattern, and hear the chapter connection.
  • Then project all 10 words. "These are your spelling words for the week — all from Chapters 5–10. With your partner: predict which 3 appear in Chapter 5. Write your top 3 on the whiteboard. You need a reason — not a guess."
  • Do NOT define every word yet. "Hold your predictions. You're about to read. You'll know if you were right soon."

5:00 — Read Chapter 5

20 min
  • Read aloud together — teacher reads the night scene for atmosphere. Students follow along.
  • Pause at: "She is Ellen. She has always been Ellen." — "Stop. What is Papa doing right now? Why does he say it like that?"
  • Pause at the photo album scene — "Why does the photograph matter? What is Papa hoping they'll see?"
  • Continue to end of chapter. Let the last lines land quietly before moving to discussion.

25:00 — I Notice / I Wonder

3 min
  • Students write one thing they noticed and one question the chapter raises.
  • Pair share for 30 seconds. This primes discussion with student thinking.

28:00 — Discussion

8 min
  • Start with student wonderings.
  • If needed: "Why did Papa lie? Can lying be a principled choice? Is there a difference between a lie that protects and a lie that harms?"
  • "The congregation's synagogue is closed. What right is being taken? How does this connect to our LOIs?"
  • Do not resolve. "There are no easy answers here. That's why this book matters."

36:00 — Quick Write: Protection and Responsibility

9 min
  • Prompt: How does Annemarie's family protect Ellen in Chapter 5? What does this show about responsibility?
  • Students write 4–5 sentences. Encourage them to use at least one spelling word when it fits naturally, such as terrified, photograph, or suspicious.
  • Quick share: ask two students to read one sentence that uses evidence from the chapter.
RULER Moment — Fear · Trust · Courage

After finishing the chapter: "The soldiers are gone. Annemarie is lying in the dark next to Ellen. What do you think she's feeling right now — not what you think she should feel, but what she actually feels?" Fear and courage often exist at exactly the same moment. That's what this chapter is showing us.

Differentiation
Support

Provide a sentence frame: "In Chapter 5, [character name] chose to ___. This reveals that they are ___ because ___." Students may keep the spelling word list visible.

Extension

Write a second response from the perspective of the Nazi soldier who checked the photos. What did he see? Did he believe Papa? Use evidence from the text.

Spelling Words + Novel Study: Chapters 6–7 — "Is the Weather Good for Fishing?" and "The House by the Sea"

LP: Open-minded · ThinkerATL: Thinking — Making ConnectionsNtS Chapters 6–7ELA.5.R.1.1 · ELA.5.V.1.1
Learning Intention

I will analyze how setting shapes mood and how spelling words from the novel help reveal danger, roles, and place.

Warm-up — Interactive Quiz · Spelling Words in Context
Use the Interactive Quiz from the spelling page. Students match clues to spelling words, then connect the words to the chapter. Today's reading focus words are suspicious, fisherman, and meadow.
suspicious
Chapter 6 — danger and doubt
Use this word to think about the soldiers' questions and the way people must hide the truth to stay safe.
fisherman
Chapter 6 — role in the system
Uncle Henrik's work matters because ordinary roles can become part of an organized system of protection.
meadow
Chapter 7 — setting and mood
This setting word helps students notice how the countryside can feel peaceful and unsafe at the same time.
How does the countryside feel different from Copenhagen — and what does that contrast tell us about what safety means in this story?
The soldiers on the train make the girls feel watched and unsafe. How does this scene build suspicion and fear?
Peter arrives in Chapter 7. Why does Lowry introduce him now, and what questions does his arrival raise for the reader?
Materials📖 Number the Stars — Chapters 6–7 · ELA notebooks🔤 Spelling Practice Page
Today we will continue using our spelling words as tools for reading closely. Chapters 6 and 7 move us away from Copenhagen and into the countryside, but the danger does not disappear. We will think about coded language, the role of Uncle Henrik as a fisherman, and the way the setting by the sea can feel peaceful and unsafe at the same time. Words like suspicious, fisherman, and meadow will help us notice how Lowry builds meaning through roles, place, mood, and what characters understand without everything being said directly.
Learner ProfileOpen-minded — Annemarie is in an entirely new environment: a farm, a way of life she doesn't know, a war that looks different here than in the city.
Thinker — Students analyze how an author uses setting strategically, not just descriptively, to build meaning.
ATL Skill — Thinking: Making ConnectionsConnecting the emotional shift in the setting to the larger narrative arc. Students who can say "Lowry describes the sea to make us feel temporary safety — because we know safety doesn't last" are making a text-to-structure connection.
Spelling Words as Reading ToolsSuspicious helps students name danger and doubt, fisherman helps them see how ordinary roles become part of an organized protection system, and meadow helps them analyze setting and mood.
Lesson Flow

0:00 — Interactive Quiz: Spelling Words in Context

5 min
  • Open the Interactive Quiz from the spelling page. Complete 5 questions whole-class, or let students answer on their own devices if available.
  • After each answer, ask: "Where might this word help us today? Danger, role, setting, mood, or responsibility?"
  • Focus words for today's reading: suspicious, fisherman, and meadow.

5:00 — Read Chapters 6–7

23 min
  • Teacher reads Ch. 6 aloud for atmosphere. Pause at Papa's coded phone call and the train scene.
  • Pause at the soldiers on the train: "Where do you see suspicion or danger in this scene? What does Annemarie understand without being told directly?"
  • Ch. 7: students read independently or with a partner. Specific task: "Mark one moment that surprises you and write one question it raises."

28:00 — Socratic Discussion

12 min
  • Students open the discussion. Teacher facilitates.
  • If discussion stalls: "Is the farm actually safer, or does it just feel that way?" / "What do we know about Peter by the end of Ch. 7?" / "How do fisherman, meadow, and suspicious help us understand setting, role, and danger?"

40:00 — Connect–Extend–Challenge

5 min
  • Connect: What did today connect to something you already knew?
  • Extend: What new idea did this class open up?
  • Challenge: What still puzzles you?
RULER Moment — Relief · Unease · Belonging

After discussing the farm: "When Annemarie arrives at Uncle Henrik's farm, write one word for what you'd feel arriving somewhere that seems safe when everything behind you was dangerous." Hold that feeling, because the novel is about to test it.

Differentiation
Support

Provide a two-column organizer: Copenhagen / The Farm. Students list 3 words that describe each setting and 1 word that describes how Annemarie feels in each.

Extension

Write a paragraph explaining why a novelist would reveal information slowly, and what effect that pacing creates in the reader.

Period 3 · ELA Writing Workshop · 45 min

Poetry — Speaking the Unspeakable

LP: Communicator · ReflectiveATL: Communication — Creative ExpressionELA.5.C.1.2Spelling words in context
Learning Intention

I will write a poem with a clear speaker's voice, intentional word choices, and at least one structural device — using our unit's big ideas as the engine.

What can a poem say that a paragraph cannot?
Who is speaking in your poem? What do they see, feel, do — or fail to do?
How does repeating a phrase or image change the emotional weight of a poem?
Today our spelling words move into writing. We will use poetry to explore ideas that are difficult to explain in ordinary paragraphs: fear, silence, responsibility, protection, and what it means to witness injustice. We will study how repetition, speaker, and carefully chosen words can create emotional weight. Then you will write from inside a moment, a character, or a perspective connected to Number the Stars or our unit. The goal is not to force rhymes. The goal is to make every word earn its place, including at least two of this week’s spelling words.
Learner ProfileCommunicator — Poetry demands precision. Students learn that every word earns its place.
Reflective — Choosing a speaker requires students to ask: "What was it really like to be in that moment?"
ATL Skill — Communication: Creative ExpressionStudents use structural craft to create emotional effect — not just to express ideas, but to make the reader feel them.
UOI Connection — Bystander / Upstander / ResponsibilityThe poetry workshop connects directly to individual responsibility. Don't over-explain it — let the poem do the work, then ask: "What does this connect to in our unit?"
Lesson Flow

0:00 — Sentence Practice + Quickwrite Provocation

5 min
  • Open the Sentence Practice section from the spelling page. Choose one word that fits poetry today: terrified, mourning, scattered, or suspicious.
  • Students write one strong sentence in context. Then quickwrite: "45 seconds — Silent witness. Go."

5:00 — Anchor Text

6 min
  • Read a short mentor poem aloud once. Silence after. "What did you notice? What hit you?"
  • Read a second time for repetition, speaker, and line breaks.

11:00 — Unpack the Craft

7 min
  • Teach anaphora as repeated structure.
  • Discuss speaker: Who is talking? What do they feel responsible for?
  • Discuss line breaks and the final turn.

18:00 — UOI Connection + Writing Invitation

3 min
  • "Today you're going to write from inside that moment. Pick your speaker — someone in the story, or someone watching from outside it."
  • Show the spelling word bank. "Use them as a writer."

21:00 — Teacher Model

4 min
  • Write 4–5 lines live as a character. Think aloud as you choose words.
  • Example: "First they came for the children of the synagogue, / and I closed my curtains — terrified at first, / then all at once, with a suspicious, practiced quiet. / I told myself: this is not my mourning."
  • "Notice: I used terrified, suspicious, and mourning. Those words earned their place because they carry the exact feeling I needed."

25:00 — Independent Writing

15 min
  • Minimum 8 lines. Must include a clear speaker's voice, at least one repeated phrase or image, and at least 2 spelling words.
  • No forced rhymes.

40:00 — Share + Reflect

5 min
  • 3–4 volunteers read one part aloud.
  • Below the poem, students write: Feel, Think, Do.

LP: Caring · PrincipledATL: Thinking — Author's CraftNtS Chapters 8–9ELA.5.R.1.3 · ELA.5.V.1.1
Learning Intention

I will analyze how Lowry builds tension and uses irony in Chapters 8–9, using spelling words from the chapter to support my thinking.

Warm-up — Spelling Practice · Words in Focus Today
casket
Chapter 8
A central object in a funeral that is not what it seems.
mourning
Chapter 8
The sadness and ritual connected to loss. In this chapter, it is also performed for protection.
suspicious
Chapter 9
Feeling that something is wrong or that someone is hiding the truth.
Aunt Birte never existed. Why would Lowry invent her?
The Nazi soldiers arrive suspicious and unconvinced. What does their suspicion do to the mood of the scene?
How is Mama's lie in this chapter different from Papa's lie in Chapter 5?
Materials📖 Number the Stars — Chapters 8–9 · ELA notebooks🔤 Spelling Practice Page
Today we will read Chapters 8 and 9, where the spelling words casket, mourning, and suspicious become essential to understanding the scene. These chapters center on a funeral that is not what it seems. We will analyze how Lowry creates tension through a sealed casket, a room full of mourners, and soldiers who suspect that something is being hidden. Our focus will be author’s craft: how word choice, mood, silence, and dramatic irony make the reader feel the danger of the moment.
Learner ProfilePrincipled — The fake funeral is an act of moral courage dressed as ritual. Discuss when a lie can protect something more important than literal truth.
Caring — The Jewish families in that room are terrified. The Danish families are risking their lives. Caring in this chapter is physical, immediate, and dangerous.
ATL Skill — Thinking: Analyzing Author's CraftThis is rich for craft analysis: irony, tension, imagery, and dramatic irony. Students move from "what happened" to "how the author made it feel that way."
Spelling Words — Suspicious, Mourning, CasketCasket and mourning reveal the ritual being performed, while suspicious names the danger that threatens the people gathered in the farmhouse.
Lesson Flow

0:00 — Spelling Practice

5 min
  • Open the Spelling Practice section. Focus on casket, mourning, suspicious, terrified, and scattered.
  • Students type each answer from a clue and receive immediate feedback.
  • Ask: "Which of these words already tells us that this chapter will be tense? Why?"

5:00 — Read Chapters 8–9

21 min
  • Teacher reads Chapter 8 aloud slowly.
  • Pause at the sealed casket: "There is no Aunt Birte. What might be happening?" Do not confirm.
  • Chapter 9: pause when soldiers arrive. "They are suspicious. What details make the room feel dangerous even before anyone opens the casket?"

26:00 — Discussion

10 min
  • "Why did Lowry invent Aunt Birte?"
  • "Everyone is in mourning, but for different things. What are people actually mourning?"
  • "Compare Papa's lie in Chapter 5 to Mama's lie in Chapter 9."

36:00 — Close Reading Annotation

9 min
  • Circle one word that creates tension.
  • Underline one detail that is more frightening than it seems.
  • Box the word suspicious. Write: how does this word increase the tension of the scene?
RULER Moment — Grief · Fear · Resolve

Before the close reading: "That room — the darkness, the casket, the gathered families — what emotion is in that room? Not one word — all of them." Lowry puts those emotions into the scene without naming all of them directly.

Differentiation
Support

Provide a tension analysis sentence frame: "In the scene where ___, Lowry creates tension by ___. The word ___ is especially powerful because ___."

Extension

Explain how dramatic irony builds suspense. Identify what the reader knows versus what the soldiers know.

Spelling Test + Novel Study: Chapter 10 — "Let Us Open the Casket"

LP: Risk-taker · PrincipledATL: Thinking — Predicting + EvaluatingNtS Chapter 10ELA.5.R.1.1 · ELA.5.L.1.1
Learning Intention

I will demonstrate mastery of this week's spelling words and analyze how Lowry shifts the story's focus to Annemarie at the end of Chapter 10, preparing for the climax.

Chapter 10 ends with Annemarie holding responsibility. What is the significance of this moment in the novel's structure?
How does Annemarie move from fear into action at a moment of great pressure?
What is Annemarie afraid of — and what does she do with that fear?
Materials📝 Spelling test paper · 📖 Number the Stars — Chapter 10 · ELA notebooks🔤 Spelling Practice Page
Today we will begin with the spelling test and then read Chapter 10, "Let Us Open the Casket." This chapter brings together many of the words and ideas we have studied this week: fear, suspicion, mourning, the casket, and people being scattered or hidden for protection. We will look at how Lowry builds suspense and how the focus of the story begins to shift toward Annemarie’s own responsibility. By the end of class, you will think about how Annemarie is changing and make an evidence-based prediction about what may happen next.
Learner ProfileRisk-taker — Annemarie takes a risk at the end of Chapter 10. This is risk-taking with thoughtfulness, not recklessness.
Principled — She acts because something morally important is at stake.
ATL Skill — Thinking: Predicting and EvaluatingStudents make a text-based prediction for Chapter 11, grounded in character development and plot structure.
Spelling Words in the ChapterScattered, terrified, casket, and suspicious can all be used to explain the pressure of Chapter 10 and Annemarie's movement toward responsible action.
