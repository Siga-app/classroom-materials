Period 1 · UOI

Unit Launch: What's the Power Behind the System?  🖼 thumbnail

🎯 Learning Intention
I will use critical analysis to sort images into conceptual groups to show what I already think about how societies organize themselves.
📋 Instructions (student-facing)
  1. Open the Image Sort activity. Do not move anything yet — take 10 seconds to look at all 12 images and just wonder.
  2. In your group, sort all 12 images into groups based on a shared idea. Every image must be placed. Write a one-sentence title for each group.
  3. Be ready to justify every placement: Why does this image belong here? What idea connects them?
  4. Relabel your groups using the three Key Concepts: Function, Causation, Responsibility.
  5. In your UOI notebook, respond: Which image best represents the Central Idea to you right now, and why? (1 sentence — no right answer.)
  6. Exit ticket: Write one question this image sort made you want to investigate.

Period 2 · UOI

Co-Constructing Our Learning Goals  🖼 thumbnail

🎯 Learning Intention
I will translate the unit's learning goals into personal "I Can" statements, taking ownership of what I want to learn and making a commitment to myself as a learner.
📋 Instructions (student-facing)
  1. Look at the KUD statements on the board. Individually, mark each one K, U, or D based on your first impression — no discussion yet.
  2. Your group is assigned one category. Rewrite each statement as a clear "I Can…" statement. Rule: if a Year 1 student couldn't understand it, rewrite it.
  3. Share your "I Can" statements with the class. Vote on the strongest version of each. Refine together: is it measurable? Ambitious enough?
  4. Copy the final "I Can" statements into your UOI notebook. Circle your Top 3 most challenging goals.
  5. Write one sentence: I commit to… because…

Period 3 · ELA

Number the Stars: Introduction + Chapters 1–2

🎯 Learning Intention
I will begin reading Number the Stars and use annotation codes to analyse how Lois Lowry shows — not tells — what it feels like to live inside a system of occupation.
📋 Instructions (student-facing)
  1. We'll look at 10 new words from the novel — write each one, identify one morphological feature (prefix, suffix, root, or origin), and share: which word surprised you most?
  2. Brief historical frame: Denmark, 1943. German occupation. Who is Lois Lowry and why did she write this?
  3. Read-aloud: Chapter 1. Pencils down, just listen. We stop when the soldiers confront Annemarie and Ellen. Freeze — what just happened?
  4. Annotate Chapter 2 independently using these codes:
    S = Setting detail that shows the system
    C = Character response to the system
    Q = Question this raises
  5. Discussion: How does Lowry make us feel the occupation without ever explaining it? Connect to Key Concept: Function.
  6. Exit: One sentence — The setting of Chapter 1 makes me feel ___ because ___.

Period 4 · Math

Algebraic Reasoning Diagnostic + Order of Operations Launch

🎯 Learning Intention
I will show what I already know about order of operations and begin to understand that mathematical expressions — like organized systems — have rules that must be followed in the correct order, or the meaning changes entirely.
📋 Instructions (student-facing)
  1. Diagnostic (10 min): 6 order of operations problems, increasing complexity. Work silently. This is not a test — it shows me where we start.
  2. Concept hook: 3 + 4 × 2. What's the answer? Two different rules give two different answers. Reveal: in mathematics, just like in government, the order of the rules matters. → PEMDAS.
  3. Think about it: the Key Concept is Function. What is the function of each operation in an expression? What happens when you change the order?
  4. Work through 4 context problems using real data from occupied Denmark. Solve and discuss: what does the correct order of operations tell us about the real situation?
  5. Exit ticket: (1) Evaluate one expression. (2) Identify the error in a worked example.
