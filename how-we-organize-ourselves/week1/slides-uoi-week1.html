Period 2 · Our Learning Goals · KNOW
By the end of this unit, we will KNOW...
How the U.S. government is organized — three branches, the Constitution, and the Bill of Rights.
What the Holocaust was and what antisemitism means.
The causes, key events, and effects of WWII — including the German occupation of Denmark.
What makes a historical recount different from a story — and how to write one.
Key vocabulary: democracy, occupation, propaganda, resistance, antisemitism, bystander, rescuer, upstander, legislation, veto.
Math: order of operations, numerical patterns & input/output tables, LCM & GCF, and integers.
Period 2 · Our Learning Goals · UNDERSTAND
By the end of this unit, we will UNDERSTAND that...
The systems societies build reflect their values — and they protect or fail their people based on those values. (Central Idea)
Democratic governments are designed to protect individual rights — but those protections depend on citizens who act with courage and principle.
History's greatest injustices happened when systems of power were built around hatred and dehumanization — and when ordinary people chose to stay silent.
Every individual within a system has rights AND responsibilities. The choice between bystander and upstander is a moral, civic, and spiritual one.
Mathematical patterns and algebraic thinking are tools for organizing the world — just as laws and constitutions are tools for organizing society.
Period 2 · Our Learning Goals · DO
By the end of this unit, we will be able to DO...
Analyze literary texts and historical sources to find theme, character development, and author's perspective.
Compare and contrast primary and secondary sources on the same historical topic.
Write a historically grounded recount using narrative techniques, descriptive detail, and accurate sequencing.
Use critical thinking to evaluate the ethical and civic dimensions of historical events.
Solve multi-step real-world problems using algebraic reasoning — including writing, evaluating, and interpreting expressions and equations.
Take principled, caring action through service — Letters Across Time.

Period 9 · Primary Source Analysis · 15 min
Annotate the Bill of Rights.
You have a short excerpt from Amendments 1, 4, and 6. For each amendment, annotate:
1
What right does this protect?
Put it in your own words. What can a person do because of this amendment?
2
Who does it protect you from?
Hint: it's not other citizens. Look at the language carefully.
3
What would a government have to do to remove this right?
Think carefully. Keep this question in mind as we read further.
Discussion + Bridge · 10 min
Why would the founders design a system that makes it hard for any one branch to act alone?

What were they afraid of?

What systems would it need to organize?

Period 13 · The Big Question · 5 min
The Declaration gives citizens the right to resist a government that fails them.

But what does that actually look like?
In Denmark, 1943 — citizens couldn't vote the Nazis out. They couldn't march in the streets.
So what did they do? And what does responsible citizenship require of you — today?

That's what we're about to discuss.
Period 13 · Collaborative Discussion · 20 min
Your group has 4 roles. Everyone has a job.
Each round is 5 minutes. After each round, rotate one position clockwise.
🗣
Speaker
Start the discussion. Share your group's first idea. Keep it going when it slows down.
❓
Questioner
Push the thinking deeper. Ask "Why?" or "What if...?" or "Can you give an example?"
✏️
Recorder
Write down the strongest ideas your group agrees on. Use the handout. One idea per line.
🔗
Synthesiser
At the end of the round: summarise in one sentence what your group decided or discovered.
Before you start
Decide who starts in each role. After each 5-minute round, everyone moves one seat clockwise — your role changes, the question changes.
Period 13 · Collaborative Discussion · Rounds
Three questions. Five minutes each.
Recorder writes in the UOI notebook. Synthesiser closes each round with one sentence.
1
"The Declaration says citizens can alter or abolish a failing government. What did Danish citizens do instead?"
5 min · rotate after
2
"Is voting the only form of civic participation? What other forms exist — legal or otherwise?"
5 min · rotate after
3
"What is the relationship between civic participation and personal risk? Is it always a choice?"
5 min · no rotation needed
Period 13 · Coming Up · Summative Preview
Letters Across Time.
Imagine you are a child in Copenhagen, 1943. You have just watched your neighbors disappear in the night — and survived because someone hid your family. You are writing to someone in the future: to us, today.
Your letter has three things:
Narrator — a child living through it, writing from inside the moment.
Recipient — a reader today who still has the chance to choose.
Purpose — solidarity and witness. This happened. Never again.
